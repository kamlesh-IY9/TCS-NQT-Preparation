## SECTION 1: PERCENTAGES (45 MINUTES) - HIGHEST PRIORITY

### Formulas to Remember

```
Percentage = (Part / Whole) × 100

Percentage Change = ((New Value - Old Value) / Old Value) × 100

Percentage Increase/Decrease = (Change / Original) × 100

Compound Percentage = multiply individual percentages
```

### Quick Concept

- If something increases by X%, and then decreases by Y%, the net result is **NOT zero**
- Always work on the **new base** after each operation

---

### Example 1: Basic Percentage

**Problem:** A product costs Rs. 500. Its price increases by 20%. What is the new price?

**Solution:**
```
Increase = 20% of 500 = (20/100) × 500 = 100
New Price = 500 + 100 = 600

Or directly: 500 × (1 + 20/100) = 500 × 1.20 = 600
```

**Answer:** Rs. 600

---

### Example 2: TCS-Level Trick Question ⚠️

**Problem:** The price of an item increased by 10%, then decreased by 10%. What is the net percentage change?

*This is a TRICK question. Many think it's 0%, but it's NOT!*

**Solution:**
```
Initial Price = 100 (assume for easy calculation)
After 10% increase = 100 × 1.10 = 110
After 10% decrease = 110 × 0.90 = 99
Net change = 99 - 100 = -1
Net percentage change = (-1 / 100) × 100 = -1%
```

**Answer:** -1% (i.e., a net DECREASE of 1%)

**KEY INSIGHT:** When you have successive percentage changes, **MULTIPLY, don't add**.

---

### Example 3: TCS-Level Reverse Percentage

**Problem:** If A is 25% more than B, then B is what percentage less than A?

**Solution:**
```
Let B = 100
A = B + 25% of B = 100 + 25 = 125

Difference = 125 - 100 = 25
B is less by 25 out of 125
Percentage = (25 / 125) × 100 = 20%
```

**Answer:** B is 20% less than A.

**KEY INSIGHT:** Percentage changes in different directions are **NOT equal**!

## SECTION 2: PROFIT & LOSS (45 MINUTES)

### Formulas to Remember

```
Cost Price (CP) = Price at which product is bought
Selling Price (SP) = Price at which product is sold

Profit = SP - CP
Loss = CP - SP

Profit % = (Profit / CP) × 100
Loss % = (Loss / CP) × 100

SP = CP × (1 + Profit% / 100)     [For Profit]
SP = CP × (1 - Loss% / 100)       [For Loss]
```

**KEY INSIGHT:** Profit % is **ALWAYS** calculated on Cost Price, not Selling Price.

---

### Example 1: Basic Profit Calculation

**Problem:** A shopkeeper buys a pen for Rs. 10 and sells it for Rs. 14. What is his profit percentage?

**Solution:**
```
CP = 10
SP = 14
Profit = 14 - 10 = 4
Profit % = (4 / 10) × 100 = 40%
```

**Answer:** 40% profit

---

### Example 2: TCS-Level Complex P&L

**Problem:** A vendor sells apples at a profit of 20%. If he had bought them at 10% less cost, and sold at the same price, what would be his profit percentage?

**Solution:**
```
Let original CP = 100
Original SP = 100 × 1.20 = 120

New CP = 100 × (1 - 10/100) = 100 × 0.90 = 90
SP remains = 120 (same as before)

New Profit = 120 - 90 = 30
New Profit % = (30 / 90) × 100 = 33.33%
```

**Answer:** 33.33% profit (increased from 20%)

---

### Example 3: TCS-Level Multiple Items

**Problem:** A shopkeeper bought 100 apples at Rs. 2 each and 50 oranges at Rs. 3 each. He sold all apples at Rs. 2.50 each and all oranges at Rs. 2.50 each. What is his overall profit/loss percentage?

**Solution:**
```
CP of apples = 100 × 2 = 200
CP of oranges = 50 × 3 = 150
Total CP = 200 + 150 = 350

SP of apples = 100 × 2.50 = 250
SP of oranges = 50 × 2.50 = 125
Total SP = 250 + 125 = 375

Total Profit = 375 - 350 = 25
Profit % = (25 / 350) × 100 = 7.14%
```

**Answer:** 7.14% profit

---

## SECTION 3: AVERAGES & ALLIGATIONS (45 MINUTES)

### Formulas to Remember

```
Average = Sum of all items / Number of items

When an item is added:
New Average = (Old Sum + New Item) / (Old Count + 1)

When an item is removed:
New Average = (Old Sum - Removed Item) / (Old Count - 1)

ALLIGATION (Mixing):
If mixing A at cost p1 and B at cost p2 to get mixture at cost pm:
Ratio = (p2 - pm) : (pm - p1)
```

---

### Example 1: Basic Average

**Problem:** The average of 5 numbers is 20. What is their sum?

**Solution:**
```
Average = Sum / Count
20 = Sum / 5
Sum = 20 × 5 = 100
```

**Answer:** 100

---

### Example 2: TCS-Level Average with Removal

**Problem:** The average weight of 10 students is 50 kg. When one student leaves, the average becomes 49 kg. What was the weight of the student who left?

**Solution:**
```
Original sum = 10 × 50 = 500 kg
New sum = 9 × 49 = 441 kg
Weight of student who left = 500 - 441 = 59 kg
```

**Answer:** 59 kg

---

### Example 3: TCS-Level Alligation (Mixing)

**Problem:** How many kg of rice costing Rs. 5 per kg should be mixed with rice costing Rs. 8 per kg to get 50 kg of rice costing Rs. 6 per kg?

**Solution:**
```
Using Alligation:
Cost of rice 1 = 5
Cost of rice 2 = 8
Cost of mixture = 6

Ratio = (8 - 6) : (6 - 5) = 2 : 1

Quantity of Rs. 5 rice = (2 / 3) × 50 = 33.33 kg
Quantity of Rs. 8 rice = (1 / 3) × 50 = 16.67 kg
```

**Answer:** Mix 33.33 kg of Rs. 5/kg rice with 16.67 kg of Rs. 8/kg rice

## SECTION 5: RATIO & PROPORTION (45 MINUTES)

### Formulas to Remember

```
Ratio: a : b means a/b (like 3:4 means 3/4)

If a : b = 3 : 4, then:
  a = 3k and b = 4k (for some constant k)

Proportion: If a/b = c/d, then a : b :: c : d

Direct Proportion: If x increases, y increases proportionally (y = kx)
Inverse Proportion: If x increases, y decreases proportionally (xy = k)
```

---

### Example 1: Basic Ratio

**Problem:** The ratio of boys to girls in a class is 3:2. If there are 30 boys, how many girls are there?

**Solution:**
```
Boys : Girls = 3 : 2
Let boys = 3k and girls = 2k
Given, 3k = 30
k = 10
Girls = 2k = 2 × 10 = 20
```

**Answer:** 20 girls

---

### Example 2: TCS-Level Ratio with Constraint

**Problem:** A sum of money is divided among A, B, and C in the ratio 2:3:5. If C's share is Rs. 1000 more than A's share, find the total sum.

**Solution:**
```
A : B : C = 2 : 3 : 5
Let A = 2k, B = 3k, C = 5k

C - A = 1000
5k - 2k = 1000
3k = 1000
k = 1000/3

Total = A + B + C = 2k + 3k + 5k = 10k
Total = 10 × (1000/3) = 10000/3 = 3333.33
```

**Answer:** Rs. 3333.33

---

### Example 3: Inverse Proportion

**Problem:** If 12 workers can complete a job in 8 days, how many days will it take 6 workers?

**Solution:**
```
Work = constant
Workers × Days = Work
12 × 8 = W
W = 96

For 6 workers:
6 × D = 96
D = 96/6 = 16 days
```

**Answer:** 16 days

---

## SECTION 6: TIME, SPEED & DISTANCE (60 MINUTES)

### Formulas to Remember

```
Distance = Speed × Time
Speed = Distance / Time
Time = Distance / Speed

Average Speed = Total Distance / Total Time

When traveling at two different speeds:
  Time ratio is INVERSE of speed ratio
```

---

### Example 1: Basic Speed Calculation

**Problem:** A car travels 300 km in 6 hours. What is its speed?

**Solution:**
```
Speed = Distance / Time = 300 / 6 = 50 km/h
```

**Answer:** 50 km/h

---

### Example 2: TCS-Level Tricky Average Speed ⚠️

**Problem:** A person travels from City A to City B at 60 km/h and returns at 40 km/h. What is the average speed for the entire journey?

**TRICK:** It's NOT (60 + 40)/2 = 50 km/h!

**Solution:**
```
Let distance between cities = d
Time going = d/60
Time returning = d/40

Total distance = 2d
Total time = d/60 + d/40 = (2d + 3d)/120 = 5d/120 = d/24

Average speed = Total distance / Total time = 2d / (d/24) = 2d × (24/d) = 48 km/h
```

**Answer:** 48 km/h (NOT 50!)

**KEY INSIGHT:** When distances are same but speeds differ, average speed is the **HARMONIC MEAN**, not arithmetic mean.

---

### Example 3: Circular Track Problem

**Problem:** Two runners A and B run around a circular track. A completes one lap in 5 minutes, and B completes one lap in 7 minutes. If they start together, when will they be together again?

**Solution:**
```
A's speed = 1/5 lap per minute
B's speed = 1/7 lap per minute

Relative speed = 1/5 - 1/7 = (7-5)/(5×7) = 2/35 lap per minute

To complete 1 lap difference: Time = 1 / (2/35) = 35/2 = 17.5 minutes
```

**Answer:** After 17.5 minutes
